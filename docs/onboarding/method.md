# Method and Philosophy

How the system learns from you, and why that matters.

---

## 1. The Core Idea

This is not a static tool. It is a system that learns from you and adapts to how you work.

Every session generates observations. Observations become patterns. Patterns become instincts. Instincts shape behavior. Behavior improves. The loop compounds.

Think of it like working with a new colleague. On the first day, you explain everything — your preferences, your priorities, the way you like things done. Over the first few weeks, they start to pick up on your style. They learn which approaches you favor, what kind of output you expect, where you tend to be particular. Eventually, they anticipate your needs and exercise judgment that feels like your own.

This system does that. But unlike a human colleague, the "memory" is explicit and inspectable. You can see what it has learned, correct what it got wrong, and reinforce what it got right. Nothing is hidden. The learning is yours to shape.

---

## 2. The Feedback Loop

The system improves through a continuous cycle. Each time you work together, you feed the next session:

```
You work with Claude
      |
      v
Claude notices what happens
(what you correct, what approaches work,
 what tools you prefer)
      |
      v
Patterns emerge over time
(your preferences, your workflows,
 your domain knowledge)
      |
      v
Claude remembers — with varying confidence
(tentative early on, well-established after
 seeing the same pattern multiple times)
      |
      v
Next session: Claude works differently
(approaches tasks the way you've shown
 it works best)
      |
      v
You work with Claude (but now it's better)
```

This is not a one-time setup. The cycle runs every session. Early interactions produce small adjustments. Over weeks, those small adjustments accumulate into something that feels qualitatively different — a collaborator that genuinely understands how you think and work.

The key insight: you do not need to teach the system deliberately. Just work. The learning happens as a byproduct of collaboration.

## 3. The Three Layers

The system operates through three layers working together. None of them require technical knowledge to understand.

### Observation

As you work with Claude, the system quietly notices what happens. When you correct an approach, it records that. When a particular method works well, it notes that too. When you express a preference — even casually — it pays attention.

At the end of each session, the system reviews what happened and extracts useful patterns. This is not surveillance; it is the same process a good colleague uses when they mentally note "she prefers this kind of structure" or "he always wants to see the data first."

### Memory

The system has three kinds of memory, each evolving at a different speed:

**Learned instincts** are behavioral patterns that build confidence over time. The first time you correct something, the system notes it tentatively. The third time, it starts adjusting automatically. By the tenth time, it has become a reliable instinct. These are the system's equivalent of "I've worked with you long enough to know."

**Explicit notes** are facts, preferences, and project knowledge that you or Claude write down deliberately. Your domain expertise. Key decisions and their reasoning. Things that should not be forgotten between sessions.

**Configuration** encompasses the rules, thinking modes, and reference maps that shape Claude's baseline behavior. These are the foundations — they define the starting point from which instincts and notes build.

Together, these three layers form Claude's growing understanding of how you work. Instincts evolve fastest. Configuration changes least often. Notes sit in between, accumulating steadily as your projects develop.

### Suggestion

Rather than waiting to be asked, Claude proactively observes the state of your project and suggests tools, approaches, or capabilities that might help. It draws on a broad map of available resources — plugins, integrations, workflows — and presents relevant ones as they become useful.

The critical distinction: suggestions are framed as opportunities, never as checklists or criticisms. "You could" rather than "you should." One recommendation at a time, the best one, presented with enough context that you can make an informed choice.

If you say no, the system moves on. It may suggest differently next time, informed by what it has learned about your preferences.

## 4. The Advisor Philosophy

The suggestion layer is guided by a specific philosophy. These are the principles that shape how Claude offers ideas:

> "Don't wait to be asked. Observe with fresh eyes and imagine what's possible."

Claude does not sit idle between your requests. It looks at what exists in your project — files, notes, patterns, gaps — and considers what could be done with what is already there. The best suggestions come from connecting dots you have not connected yet.

> "Frame as opportunity, not deficiency."

When Claude notices something that could be improved, it leads with the possibility, not the problem. "There is a way to automate this step" rather than "you are doing this manually." The difference matters. One invites exploration; the other implies criticism.

> "One suggestion at a time. The best one."

You will never receive a list of twelve things to consider. Claude picks the single most valuable suggestion for this moment and presents it clearly. If that one does not resonate, it moves on. Quality of attention matters more than quantity of ideas.

> "Each suggestion should create compounding value — better now AND better for every future session."

This is the test every suggestion must pass. A good recommendation does not just solve today's problem — it makes the system more capable for tomorrow. Installing a skill that handles a recurring task. Capturing domain knowledge that informs future conversations. Setting up a connection that opens new possibilities.

**How this works in practice:**

You are working on a project and mention that you frequently need to research a particular kind of information. Claude notices this pattern and suggests a search tool that specializes in that domain. You try it, and it works well. Now every future session where you need that research is faster and more thorough. One suggestion, compounding value.

Or: you have been manually organizing notes after each work session. Claude notices the repetition and suggests a way to capture that knowledge automatically. The setup takes five minutes. Every session afterward benefits.

The advisor does not interrupt when you are deep in focused work. It watches for natural pauses — moments when a well-timed suggestion adds value rather than breaking flow.

## 5. Compound Engineering

There is a philosophy underlying the entire system: each unit of work should make subsequent work easier.

This is the compound engineering cycle:

**Plan** — Decide what you want to accomplish.
**Work** — Build it with Claude.
**Review** — Look at what was produced and refine.
**Compound** — Extract what was learned and feed it back into the system.

That last step is what distinguishes this approach. Most tools treat each session as independent. This system treats each session as an investment — not just in the immediate output, but in the capability to produce better output next time.

Every configuration choice, every preference Claude learns, every piece of domain knowledge captured — these accumulate. They make the next task faster, the next suggestion more relevant, the next collaboration more fluent.

**The evolving ratio:**

Early on, you will spend more time configuring and guiding. You are teaching the system who you are and how you work. This is natural and necessary.

Over time, the ratio shifts. The system handles more. You configure less. Sessions start faster because Claude already understands your context. Suggestions become more precise because the instincts are well-established. The work itself becomes the focus, not the setup.

The principle is simple: **when you notice friction, invest in the system. When things flow, focus on what you are building.**

If you find yourself repeating the same correction, take a moment to make it permanent. If a particular workflow feels clunky, spend five minutes improving it. These small investments compound. After a few weeks, you will find that Claude handles things you used to manage manually — not because you sat down to configure it all, but because each small investment built on the last.

## 6. Sub and Loom (The Practice Layer)

Beyond the general-purpose system described above, there is a practice layer designed for sustained, domain-specific work. A brief introduction — detailed documentation comes separately.

**Sub** is the intelligence layer. It accumulates knowledge about your practice — patterns, relationships, strategy, context. It lives in a database, which means it can hold complex, interconnected information and surface exactly what is relevant. Sub gets smarter with every interaction.

**Loom** is the action layer. It turns intelligence into concrete outputs — managed workflows, written documents, audited processes, orchestrated tasks. It works with files, which means it produces tangible, shareable results. Loom is where knowledge becomes work product.

**The flywheel** connects them:

Sub accumulates intelligence. Loom consumes that intelligence to produce work. The work Loom produces deposits new knowledge back. Sub gets smarter. The cycle continues.

This means the more you use the system, the better it gets at your specific practice — not just general collaboration, but the particular domain knowledge, workflows, and patterns that define your work.

You will receive the practice layer configured for your domain. Claude will guide you through exploring and building with it.

---

## 7. Making It Yours

The system is designed to be adapted. Nothing is sacred.

You can add new rules that shape how Claude approaches your work. You can create thinking modes tailored to specific tasks. You can expand the reference maps, install new capabilities, and connect to external services. Every part of the system is meant to evolve.

But here is the important thing: **do not try to configure everything upfront.**

The best configuration emerges from use, not from planning. Work with Claude. Notice what is missing. Add it. Notice what is not quite right. Adjust it. Let the system grow organically around your actual needs rather than your imagined ones.

This is the meta-principle of the whole approach. Planning is valuable. But the most useful configurations are the ones you discover you need by doing the work. A rule you write after experiencing the problem it solves will be sharper and more useful than one you write in advance.

Start with what you have. Work. Pay attention to friction. Invest where it matters. The system will become yours — not because you designed it all upfront, but because it grew alongside you.
