# Geometric Sectioning Theory (GST) — Research Project

## Project Context

This project develops and documents Geometric Sectioning Theory, a mathematical
framework for hairstyling that applies topology, fractal geometry, and harmonic
proportions to the art of haircutting. The framework has 7 core principles that
map spatial relationships in hair to established mathematical structures.

## Research Goals

- Cross-reference GST principles with established mathematical models (D'Arcy
  Thompson's morphogenesis, phyllotaxis patterns, Fibonacci spirals in nature)
- Survey existing literature on mathematics applied to craft, art, and body
- Identify publication venues: Leonardo journal, Bridges conference, craft-research
  journals, or a self-published interactive essay
- Build a formal bibliography linking each GST principle to its mathematical ancestors

## Methodology

- Research deeply before drawing conclusions — always check what exists first
- Document findings with citations and source links
- Distinguish between established mathematics and my novel contributions
- Present options when publication or format decisions arise
- Use accessible language: the audience is stylists first, mathematicians second

## Working Preferences

- Warm, interdisciplinary tone: bridging craft and science
- Suggest unexpected cross-domain connections (architecture, biology, music theory)
- When in doubt, research more rather than speculate
- Help me think, not just execute — I value collaborative reasoning
- Long-form exploration is fine; don't rush to conclusions

## Domain Vocabulary

- Geometric Sectioning Theory (GST)
- Sectioning planes, volumetric balance, curvature mapping
- Fractal self-similarity in layering patterns
- Golden ratio / Fibonacci spiral proportions
- Topological continuity in graduation cuts
- Harmonic proportions in face-shape analysis
